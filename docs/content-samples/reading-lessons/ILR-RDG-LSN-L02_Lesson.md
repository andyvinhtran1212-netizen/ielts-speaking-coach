## READING PASSAGE 1
### Learning Outdoors
*The slow return of play-based education in Northern Europe*

**A**  In 1985, a small group of Danish parents grew worried that their five-year-olds spent almost the entire school day sitting indoors. They responded by opening a kindergarten in a nearby forest, where children would learn through climbing, digging and building shelters from sticks. Four decades later, the idea has spread across Northern Europe, and today more than 1,500 forest schools operate in Denmark, Germany and the United Kingdom. Their popularity has revived an old educational debate about how much of early learning should actually take place at a desk.

**B**  Forest schools draw on a long tradition. The Italian doctor Maria Montessori, writing in the early twentieth century, argued that young children learn best when they handle real objects rather than copy from a board. The Swiss educator Johann Pestalozzi, a century earlier, made a similar case for combining lessons with outdoor activity. Forest schools combine these ideas with a Scandinavian habit known as friluftsliv, a word that roughly means "open-air life" and which treats time outdoors as essential to well-being. The result is a curriculum that looks playful but is carefully planned.

**C**  A typical morning at a forest kindergarten near Copenhagen begins with children choosing one of three or four activity zones: a building area with logs and rope, a planting area, or a small workshop for carving wood with safety knives. Teachers move between groups, asking questions and helping with tools, but they rarely give a whole-class instruction. Lunch is cooked over an open fire two or three times a week. Children are expected to dress for the weather, and in the Nordic phrase often heard at such schools, "there is no bad weather, only bad clothing."

**D**  Research on outcomes has begun to accumulate. A 2019 study by Dr Liisa Karinen, an early-childhood researcher at the University of Helsinki, followed 600 Finnish children from age four to age eight. Pupils who had attended forest-based kindergartens showed slightly better balance and stamina, as expected, but also stronger collaboration skills than peers from indoor settings. Teachers later reported that these children adapted more quickly to group work in primary school. They were no better, however, at early reading or basic arithmetic, and a smaller number struggled with the transition to sustained seated tasks. The results suggest that outdoor learning supports certain skills, particularly social and physical ones, without necessarily accelerating the acquisition of literacy.

**E**  Not everyone is convinced. Critics argue that forest schools work well in countries with strong outdoor traditions and short rainy winters, but they may be difficult to copy in urban settings where children travel long distances and parents work long hours. There is also concern about safety. Although accident rates at well-run forest schools appear to be no higher than at indoor kindergartens, headlines occasionally focus on minor injuries from climbing or fire-pits. Some parents simply prefer the visible structure of a traditional classroom, with its tables, chairs and printed worksheets.

**F**  Cost is another obstacle. Running a forest kindergarten typically requires a smaller adult-to-child ratio than indoor settings, because supervising activities such as carving and fire-cooking demands close attention. In wealthier districts, this expense is absorbed by parental fees; in poorer areas, it may need public funding that is not always available. As a result, access to forest schools has tended to follow income, and some commentators worry that an approach designed for all children may quietly become a privilege of the few.

**G**  Despite these concerns, the broader shift towards outdoor and play-based learning shows little sign of slowing. Several European education ministries now recommend at least one outdoor session per week even within mainstream primary schools, and a growing body of evidence indicates that the benefits, although modest, are real. In Scotland, a national strategy launched in 2021 set the target of weekly outdoor lessons for every primary class, and similar pilots are under way in parts of Germany and the Netherlands. Whether forest schools become a standard option or remain an attractive niche may depend less on the strength of the research and more on whether cities can rearrange the school day around children, rather than the other way around.
