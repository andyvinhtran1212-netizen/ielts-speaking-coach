### Water for a Million Romans

*The aqueducts that supplied imperial Rome were among the most ambitious engineering projects of the ancient world*

By the year 100 CE, the city of Rome was home to roughly one million people, and each of them had access to more water than the average European city dweller would receive again until the late nineteenth century. The supply was delivered by a network of eleven major aqueducts, the longest of which stretched for ninety-one kilometres. The historian Sextus Julius Frontinus, appointed water commissioner in 97 CE, recorded the system in a treatise that still survives. He observed that the volume reaching the city each day would have been impossible to imagine in any other ancient capital. The aqueducts were also, in their own time, a public proof of Roman engineering skill.

Each aqueduct began at a carefully chosen source, almost always a spring or a high mountain stream. Roman surveyors searched for water that was cold, abundant and slightly mineralised; standing water from lakes was generally avoided. Once a source had been approved, the engineers measured its elevation and traced a path back to Rome that fell at a very gentle gradient, usually between one and three metres per kilometre. The whole system depended on gravity. There were no pumps, and any major rise in the ground had to be either tunnelled through or bypassed.

Where the route met a hillside that was too steep to climb around, the workers cut a tunnel straight through the rock. Pairs of vertical shafts were sunk from the surface at regular intervals so that several teams could dig towards each other underground. The longest of these tunnels, on the Aqua Marcia, ran for more than ten kilometres beneath the Apennines. When the route crossed a wide valley, on the other hand, the channel had to be carried on a long series of arches. The arched sections were the most visible feature of the system, and the ruined arches near Rome still impress visitors today. They were also expensive, and were used only when no easier route existed.

Before the water entered the city, it passed through a settling tank known as a piscina. The tank slowed the flow so that sand, gravel and any organic matter sank to the bottom; cleaner water continued onwards from a higher outlet. Some aqueducts had more than one such tank along their length. Frontinus regarded the piscinae as essential to the quality of the supply, and noted that aqueducts without them tended to deliver water that was visibly cloudy after heavy rain.

Inside the city the water arrived at a large distribution reservoir called a castellum. From the castellum, the supply was divided into three roughly equal portions: one for public fountains, one for the imperial baths, and one for private households who had paid for a connection. Lead pipes carried the water from the castellum through the streets. Although the Romans were aware that lead could cause illness if it was eaten or breathed in as dust, they considered the risk acceptable in pipes, where the inner surface quickly became coated with calcium deposits that limited contact with the water.

Maintaining the aqueducts was a constant task. A staff of about seven hundred slaves and freedmen, known collectively as the familia aquaria, walked the routes daily, repaired cracks, removed plant roots from joints, and reported illegal connections, working in small gangs that were each responsible for a fixed length of channel. Such connections were a serious problem: any landowner whose estate lay close to the channel could secretly tap into it. Frontinus claimed to have discovered that almost a quarter of the supply was being lost to theft. Whether his figure was exact or not, the language of his treatise leaves no doubt about the scale of the abuse.

The Roman model influenced engineers for centuries. Aqueducts built on similar principles served Constantinople from the fourth century, and shorter versions were constructed in Spain, France and North Africa wherever cities outgrew their wells. After the collapse of the Western Empire, however, the long-distance supply of water to large cities became rare in Europe for almost a thousand years. The system that Frontinus described would not be matched again until the modern era. Whether such a complex public service could have survived in a less centralised society is a question that historians continue to debate.

### Running Out of Antibiotics

*Ninety years after the discovery of penicillin, the world is struggling to keep ahead of the bacteria that once made everyday infections deadly*

**A**  In 2019, drug-resistant infections were directly responsible for an estimated 1.27 million deaths worldwide, more than either malaria or HIV/AIDS in the same year. The figure, published in The Lancet in 2022, was the most comprehensive global estimate yet produced. It also confirmed something that infectious-disease specialists had long argued: the so-called 'antibiotic era', opened by Alexander Fleming's chance observation of penicillin in 1928, is now under sustained threat. Bacteria that were once routinely killed by a short course of pills are increasingly able to survive every drug a doctor can prescribe.

**B**  The biological problem behind these numbers is straightforward. Whenever a population of bacteria is exposed to an antibiotic, a small minority of cells will, by chance, carry mutations that allow them to resist the drug. They survive, multiply and pass their resistance genes on. Worse, bacteria readily share such genes across species through small mobile pieces of DNA called plasmids, so a resistance trait that arises in a harmless gut bacterium can quickly reach a dangerous hospital pathogen. The process is not new; what is new is the speed at which it now occurs, driven by the colossal volumes of antibiotics consumed in human medicine and, particularly, in industrial livestock farming.

**C**  For most of the twentieth century, the threat of resistance was offset by a steady flow of new antibiotics. Between the 1940s and the 1980s, pharmaceutical companies introduced more than twenty entirely new classes of drugs. Since 1987, however, only two such classes have reached the clinic. The reasons are partly commercial: a course of antibiotics, prescribed for a few days, is far less profitable than a drug for a chronic condition taken for life. They are also partly scientific. Soil-dwelling microbes, the source of most existing antibiotics, have been thoroughly searched, and easy candidates were exhausted decades ago. Newer leads tend to share structural features with drugs already in use, and resistance to them can emerge within months.

**D**  Faced with this pipeline problem, researchers have begun to look in unfamiliar places. Marine sediments, the guts of insects and even the lining of human noses have all yielded promising molecules. In 2016, a team led by Andreas Peschel at the University of Tübingen reported a new compound, lugdunin, produced by a bacterium that lives in the nasal passages of about one in ten people. Lugdunin proved effective against several drug-resistant strains in mice, although clinical use remains several years away. A separate approach uses machine-learning models to scan vast libraries of known chemicals for antibiotic activity. A 2020 paper from MIT described one such model that identified halicin, a molecule originally investigated for an unrelated condition, as a powerful broad-spectrum agent.

**E**  Yet new drugs alone will not solve the problem. As long as antibiotics are used carelessly, resistance will continue to spread. A growing international effort, sometimes called antimicrobial stewardship, focuses on slowing the demand side. Hospitals are encouraged to review prescriptions daily, to switch to narrower-spectrum drugs as soon as test results allow, and to remove unnecessary intravenous lines. National campaigns discourage the use of antibiotics for viral infections such as ordinary sore throats. Outside the clinic, regulators have moved to restrict the use of growth-promoting antibiotics in livestock; the European Union banned the practice in 2006 and similar rules took effect in much of Asia in the following decade.

**F**  Such measures appear to work. After Sweden tightened prescribing rules in the late 1990s, hospital infections with the most resistant strains of Staphylococcus aureus fell by about 31 per cent over the next ten years. England's national stewardship programme, introduced in 2014, has been associated with a 7 per cent reduction in total antibiotic use without any rise in mortality from the relevant infections. Critics note, however, that such achievements are easily reversed; stewardship requires constant attention from busy clinicians, and in a stretched health service the temptation to prescribe quickly is hard to resist.

**G**  A different line of work attempts to outflank bacteria altogether. Bacteriophages — viruses that infect only specific bacterial species — were used as treatments in the Soviet Union for decades and are now being re-examined in Europe and the United States. In 2019, a teenage patient at Great Ormond Street Hospital in London became one of the first in the West to be treated successfully with a genetically modified phage. The approach is highly targeted, but each phage must be matched to the patient's specific infection, which makes standard regulatory approval difficult. Other groups are exploring vaccines against common hospital pathogens, on the grounds that an infection that never occurs cannot become drug-resistant.

**H**  Most experts now agree that no single innovation will end the problem. Dame Sally Davies, the United Kingdom's former chief medical officer, has argued that resistance must be treated as a 'slow-motion pandemic' demanding the same long-term political commitment as climate change. Whether such commitment will appear is, for the moment, uncertain. What is clearer is that the casual abundance of effective antibiotics, which began in the 1940s and reshaped medicine entirely, is unlikely to be a permanent feature of the twenty-first century.

### The Strength of Slight Acquaintance

*Half a century after Mark Granovetter's celebrated paper on weak ties, sociologists are revisiting both its claims and its limits.*

**A**  In 1973, a young sociologist named Mark Granovetter published a paper with the unpromising title 'The Strength of Weak Ties'. Drawing on a small interview study of professional workers in a Boston suburb, Granovetter reported a counter-intuitive finding: when people landed a new job through a personal contact, that contact was rarely a close friend or relative. In about 83 per cent of cases the helpful acquaintance was someone the respondent saw only occasionally, or even rarely. From this observation Granovetter built a more general argument. Close friends, he suggested, tend to know the same people and circulate the same information. Weak ties — colleagues from a previous job, friends of friends, former classmates — by contrast act as bridges between otherwise unconnected groups and are therefore the most important channels of novel information. The paper went on to become one of the most cited in the social sciences.

**B**  Granovetter's intuition was elegant, but its empirical foundations were modest. The original study covered fewer than three hundred respondents in a single occupation, and the analysis relied on the respondents' own descriptions of how often they had contact with the person who had told them about the job. For two decades, the most that could be claimed was that the pattern was striking and theoretically suggestive. The arrival of large-scale digital data has now made it possible to test the claim on a very different scale. Recent studies, drawing on hundreds of millions of social-media connections and labour-market records, have largely vindicated Granovetter's original direction of travel, while also complicating it in ways he could not have anticipated.

**C**  The most thorough test to date appeared in the journal Science in 2022. Karthik Rajkumar, Iavor Bojinov and their colleagues analysed twenty separate randomised experiments conducted on LinkedIn between 2015 and 2019. Over five years, the experiments involved roughly twenty million users and produced more than two billion new connections, and the researchers were able to track which of those new ties were followed by an actual change of job. The general pattern was clear: moderately weak ties — those involving fewer mutual contacts and less frequent interaction — were several times more likely to lead to job mobility than either very strong ties or very distant ones. The very weakest ties of all, with almost no mutual contacts, produced fewer transfers, which suggests that an intermediate level of social closeness is most effective.

**D**  These results were widely reported as a vindication of Granovetter, but a number of researchers have read them more cautiously. Matthew Jackson, an economist at Stanford and the author of an influential book on networks, accepts that the LinkedIn data confirm a real effect, yet emphasises that the size of the benefit varied dramatically by industry. In high-skill fields such as software engineering, weak ties were highly predictive of job moves; in less specialised sectors, the effect was much weaker. Jackson interprets this as a reminder that networks transmit information unevenly, and that the celebrated 'strength of weak ties' may apply most clearly to occupations where novel information is itself the scarce good.

**E**  A related complication concerns the social character of those weak ties. Granovetter's original respondents were almost entirely white, male professionals. Subsequent fieldwork, particularly by the sociologist Sandra Susan Smith at the University of California, Berkeley, has shown that for low-income and minority workers, weak ties function very differently. Smith's interviews with unemployed African-American job-seekers in Michigan documented a widespread reluctance to pass on tips about jobs to mere acquaintances, partly because the recommender risked being held responsible for any subsequent failure. The structural prediction — that weak ties carry more novel information — may be correct in the aggregate, but its practical pay-off can be greatly reduced where social trust is fragile.

**F**  Researchers have also begun to ask what counts as a 'tie' in a digital age. A LinkedIn connection requires nothing more than a single click and may never be activated; a face-to-face acquaintance, by contrast, involves cumulative cues such as eye contact and shared experience. In a 2023 paper, Hanna Brückner and colleagues at the European University Institute compared self-reported friendship surveys with the same individuals' digital networks and found that almost a third of online contacts were not perceived as ties at all by the people involved. Whether the effects observed on platforms such as LinkedIn would replicate in offline settings is therefore an open question, although early evidence from longitudinal studies in Sweden suggests that the broad pattern does carry over.

**G**  A more philosophical objection has come from those who worry about the policy uses of network research. If weak ties matter so much, it is tempting to encourage their proliferation through digital platforms and networking events. But, as the political theorist Wendy Nelson Espeland has argued, treating relationships primarily as instruments of career advancement may erode the very qualities — disinterested interest, mutual obligation — that make ties meaningful in the first place. Espeland's argument echoes earlier criticisms of social-capital theory and reminds policy-makers that not everything valuable about human connection is captured by what it can deliver.

**H**  Where does this leave Granovetter's claim half a century after it first appeared? The evidence now suggests that the strength of weak ties is real but not universal, that the effect depends on the kind of information being transmitted, and that digital metrics of connection can be misleading. As Bojinov has noted in a recent interview, the most surprising lesson of the LinkedIn experiments is not that weak ties matter, which was already plausible, but that the relationship between tie strength and outcomes is non-linear: very weak and very strong ties both underperform an intermediate band. Such findings caution against any simple slogan, including the slogan that gave the field its name.
