Coral reefs are sometimes called the **cities of the sea**. A single healthy reef
can host a quarter of all known marine species, packed into a structure no larger
than a small town. Their colour comes from tiny algae that live inside the coral
and trade food for shelter — a partnership built up over thousands of years.

That partnership is now under unusual pressure. As oceans warm, the algae are
expelled and the coral turns ghostly white in a process scientists call
**bleaching**. Some reefs recover when temperatures fall again. Many do not. The
2016 and 2017 bleaching events affected more than half of the world's coral
species, and researchers say full recovery is unlikely within decades.

Beyond warming, reefs face slower threats too — plastic pollution, coastal
construction, and overfishing — but most experts now agree that heat is the
single most urgent driver. Cooling the ocean, not cleaning it, is what would
matter most.
